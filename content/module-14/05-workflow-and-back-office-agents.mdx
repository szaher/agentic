## Overview

In Lesson 04, you explored DevOps agents that automate incident response, code review, and CI/CD workflows. This lesson covers workflow and back-office agents—the engines behind document processing, approval chains, procurement, and structured business processes.

These agents handle high-volume, repetitive tasks where manual processing is slow and error-prone. Unlike research or coding agents that reason open-endedly, workflow agents follow structured processes with well-defined steps, error handling at each step, and human escalation when the process breaks down.

This lesson draws on control flow patterns from Module 5 (conditional branching, loops, error handling) and multi-agent coordination from Module 10 (delegation, handoffs, shared state).

> Workflow agents deliver value through reliable execution of known processes, not creative problem-solving.

## Structured Process Execution vs Free-Form Reasoning

Workflow agents differ fundamentally from research, coding, or analysis agents:

| Dimension | Workflow Agents | Research/Coding Agents |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Problem Type** | Well-defined, repeatable processes | Novel, open-ended problems |
| **Value Proposition** | Reliable execution, error handling, audit trail | Creative solutions, synthesis, code generation |
| **Success Metric** | Process completion rate, error rate, cycle time | Solution quality, correctness, user satisfaction |
| **Failure Mode** | Process breaks down, data invalid, rule violated | Incorrect reasoning, wrong solution, hallucination |
| **Human Role** | Define rules, approve exceptions, resolve errors | Provide goals, evaluate solutions, refine requirements |

A workflow agent's value is in consistent, auditable execution of structured steps—not in discovering new solutions or reasoning about ambiguous situations.

### When Workflows Break Down

Workflow agents escalate to humans when:

- **Data Does Not Match Rules** — An invoice amount exceeds the budget, a vendor is not in the approved list, a date is invalid.
- **External System Failure** — The ERP system is down, the approval API times out, the document storage is unavailable.
- **Ambiguous Input** — The document format is unrecognized, multiple vendors match the extracted name, the request type is unclear.
- **Policy Exception** — A purchase request needs executive approval outside the normal chain, a contract requires legal review, a refund exceeds standard limits.

In these cases, the agent hands off to a human with full context: the original request, attempted steps, error details, and recommended next actions.

## Document Processing

Document processing agents ingest, extract, classify, and validate structured information from unstructured documents: invoices, purchase orders, contracts, receipts, forms.

### The Document Processing Pipeline

1. **Ingestion** — Receive document (PDF, image, email attachment) and convert to processable format (text extraction, OCR for scanned images).

2. **Classification** — Determine document type (invoice, purchase order, receipt, contract amendment) to apply the correct extraction template.

3. **Extraction** — Extract named entities: vendor name, invoice number, line items, amounts, dates, tax IDs, purchase order references.

4. **Validation** — Check internal consistency (does the total match the sum of line items?) and business rules (is the date within fiscal year? is the vendor approved? does the amount require VP approval?).

5. **Routing** — Send the document to the next step in the workflow (approval, payment processing, archive) or flag for human review if validation fails.

This pipeline combines structured output patterns from Module 2 (extraction with JSON schemas) with domain-specific validation logic.

### Example: Invoice Processing

An invoice processing agent extracts:

- Vendor name and ID
- Invoice number
- Invoice date
- Due date
- Line items (description, quantity, unit price, total)
- Subtotal, tax, total amount
- Payment terms
- Purchase order reference (if present)

It then validates:

- Total = subtotal + tax
- Line item totals = sum of (quantity × unit price)
- Vendor ID exists in approved vendor list
- Invoice date ≤ today
- Due date > invoice date
- Purchase order reference exists (if required)

If validation passes, the invoice moves to approval routing. If validation fails, the agent flags specific errors (e.g., "Total mismatch: expected $1,234.56, got $1,235.00") and escalates to accounts payable.

## Approval Chain Orchestration

Approval workflows route requests through a chain of approvers based on business rules. Workflow agents automate routing, delegation, escalation, and segregation of duties.

### Approval Routing Rules

Common routing patterns:

- **Amount Thresholds** — Purchases under $1,000: auto-approve. $1,000–$10,000: manager approval. Over $10,000: VP approval.
- **Requester Role** — Requests from executives: expedited approval. Requests from contractors: additional verification.
- **Category-Specific Rules** — IT purchases: CTO approval. Marketing spend: CMO approval. Legal services: General Counsel approval.
- **Segregation of Duties** — The requester cannot approve their own request. The approver cannot be the requester's direct report.

### Delegation and Escalation

Delegation handles temporary unavailability:

- Approver is out of office → route to designated delegate
- Approver does not respond within 48 hours → escalate to their manager
- Request is urgent → notify approver and escalate in parallel

Escalation ensures requests do not stall:

- Pending for more than 2 business days → remind approver
- Pending for more than 5 business days → escalate to next level
- Pending for more than 10 business days → route to executive oversight

### Segregation of Duties

Segregation of duties prevents conflicts of interest. Common controls:

- **Requester ≠ Approver** — The person who submits the request cannot approve it.
- **Approver ≠ Processor** — The person who approves the payment cannot process the payment.
- **Dual Approval** — High-value transactions require two independent approvals.

Workflow agents enforce these rules automatically, rejecting or rerouting requests that violate segregation policies.

## Integration Patterns

Workflow agents integrate with enterprise systems through APIs, webhooks, or MCP servers:

- **Ticketing Systems** — Jira, ServiceNow, Zendesk — create tickets, update status, assign to users, close tickets.
- **ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning)** — SAP, Oracle, Microsoft Dynamics — read vendor records, create purchase orders, update inventory, post journal entries.
- **CRM (Customer Relationship Management)** — Salesforce, HubSpot, Zoho — read account data, update contact records, log interactions.
- **Email and Calendaring** — Outlook, Gmail, Google Calendar — send notifications, schedule approvals, parse inbound requests.
- **HRIS (Human Resources Information System)** — Workday, BambooHR — verify employee records, check manager hierarchy, enforce approval chains based on org structure.

Each integration requires:

- **Authentication** — API keys, OAuth tokens, service accounts.
- **Rate Limiting** — Respect API quotas, batch requests, retry with exponential backoff.
- **Error Handling** — Handle timeouts, invalid responses, partial failures.
- **Audit Logging** — Record every API call for compliance and debugging.

For systems without APIs, agents may use email parsing (extract structured data from email requests), web scraping (read data from internal portals), or RPA (robotic process automation) to interact with legacy UIs.

## Write-Action Autonomy Tiers for Workflow Agents

Workflow agents perform write operations (create, update, approve) on business records. Autonomy tiers match the blast radius of each operation:

    ["Read-Only Lookup", "View records, query databases, retrieve documents", "None (no state change)", "Auto-execute"],
    ["Record Creation", "Create new ticket, add document entry, log interaction", "Single record", "Auto-execute with validation"],
    ["Field Update", "Change status, update metadata, add comment", "Single record field", "Auto-execute with audit log"],
    ["Approval/Rejection", "Approve purchase request, reject refund, sign-off on review", "Workflow state + downstream effects", "Conditional auto-execute (within policy) or human approval (exception)"],
    ["Bulk Operations", "Batch status updates, mass reassignment, bulk import", "Many records", "Human approval required"]
  ]}
/>

> Higher autonomy tiers require stronger controls: validation, audit logging, human approval for exceptions, and rollback capability.

### Autonomy Level Guidelines

- **Auto-Execute** — Actions that follow strict validation rules and have low blast radius (single record, reversible, no financial impact).
- **Auto-Execute with Audit Log** — Actions that modify important fields but are reversible and within normal business operations.
- **Conditional Auto-Execute** — Actions that follow policy within normal parameters (e.g., auto-approve purchases under $1,000) but require human approval for exceptions (e.g., purchases over threshold, unapproved vendors).
- **Human Approval Required** — Actions with high blast radius (affect many records), irreversible consequences (financial commitments, legal obligations), or policy exceptions.

## Enterprise Controls Used in This Vertical

Workflow and back-office agents handle sensitive business processes: financial transactions, legal agreements, employee data. Enterprise controls ensure agents operate within policy boundaries:

- **Segregation of Duties** — Prevents conflicts of interest by enforcing role separation (requester ≠ approver, approver ≠ processor).
- **Approval Chain Integrity** — Routes requests through the correct sequence of approvers based on amount, category, and organizational hierarchy. Escalates when approvers are unavailable or unresponsive.
- **Document Retention** — Archives all processed documents (invoices, contracts, forms) with metadata (processing date, extracted fields, validation results) for compliance and audit.
- **Regulatory Audit** — Maintains immutable logs of every agent action (what was processed, when, by which agent, with what outcome) to satisfy financial, legal, and industry-specific regulations (SOX, GDPR, HIPAA).

These controls mirror the production safety patterns from Module 12: explicit authorization, comprehensive audit trails, and fail-safe defaults.

## Summary

Workflow and back-office agents automate document processing, approval chains, procurement, and structured business processes. They deliver value through reliable, auditable execution of well-defined workflows—not through creative problem-solving.

The key to safe workflow automation is autonomy tiers matched to blast radius: read-only operations auto-execute, single-record writes proceed with validation, approvals follow policy rules, and bulk operations require human approval. Segregation of duties, approval chain integrity, and comprehensive audit trails ensure agents operate within enterprise policy boundaries.
